reflections for annika

last night you showed me the movie fight club. the funny truth is that growing up in the countryside I never got to watch much television, and I guess I was disconnected from popular culture for a long time. so despite how incredibly famous this work is, I never saw it. and I really enjoyed it. I thought it was fun to watch a film with someone on the other side of a continent. I thought of you there, the red glow of your computer screen, watching something else at the same time deep into the night. the world turns on small pleasures like this: doesn’t it? it turns, the sun sets and rises in different places, and one talks and talks through it all. all the chatter of meeting someone new, the enchantment of uncovering a new facet in them, wondering how you might come across, and wondering wondering about it all. the small comfort of a phone screen, someone on the other end, carrying you through. and thinking about dreams and destinies, and half moons, and palm trees under the street lamp.

so much for prose poetry. I thought that you might want something to chew on. so I sat down and reflected for a while, and I organized it by theme. and here it is, the male reflection on the film you wondered about:

apollonian and dionysian men, and the unconquered shadow self: I took it that the films primary question was: how do men fit into apollonian civilization? the film presents the daytime tyler as default, the daytime self, endorsed by civilization proper. to work as a recall coordinator for a large corporation - what could be more respectable, more endorsed? the office scenes cast in pallid gray. sitting in his house ordering from the ikea catalog. daytime tyler is human capital. sexless. boring and safe. see what happens when he’s presented with the actual wreckage of a car accident: he considered the physicals facts of melted fat and crushed bodies as elements in a recall formula. the company, and tyler as its object, can’t (wont?) approach the emotive reality of the wreck as a tragedy that happened to individuals - the situation is consumed and aggregated into economic facts to be reasoned about, manipulated by technique (from ellul). so for the daytime tyler the economy is a hyperreality. it sits above and replaces the smaller reality that takes place within himself. capital strips him of his ability to see reality as primarily emotive. bodies are facts - his home is a collection of trinkets - his only relationship with a woman takes place as an adversarial negotiation, and meanwhile he sits alone in his home.

I think the critique of the daytime tyler, and the films identification of the daytime tyler with the appollonian world of facts and structures (essentially saying: capital is the only true reality), is basically correct. I have lived that life. I worked for two large corporations over a period of five years doing consulting. it killed me and stripped the life from me too. I felt like a rat. I worked 80 hours a week and turned gray sitting in my home. and when I quit to found a studio, I didn’t miss the money at all, I didn’t care. I never thought about it again. I remember - after I quit, I returned home to michigan. it was the fall. I was in my village. I stared up at the golden birch trees turning in the early crisp air - light shown through there. the world was bathed in golden light. and the five years of my life prior felt like a dream. which is fitting - it was the dream, the appollonian unreal dream world of relations under capital. so I woke up from the unreal reality, tragically endorsed by the economy, to the real reality that sat inside of me the whole time, which amounted more or less to an address of the way I actually felt under that system. which was miserable.

so the nighttime tyler absorbed everything that the daytime tyler couldn’t look at. it acted out the dionysian instincts by degrees. it started with pornography, started with cocks inserted in the middle of film reels, with piss in lobster bisque, with drinking and fighting. all properly freudian and base, and disgusting. and it progressed - it progressed from pornography to constant, violent fucking. it progressed from fighting in a club to holding people at gunpoint and threatening to kill them. it acted out in so many horrible ways. it went from the capital relations at the office to the informal and anonymous relations in the club, and eventually became the raising of an army, the recruiting of others, the great plan to act out a general vengeance on society as a whole.

I am familiar with the way this thrusting forth of the shadow, dionysian self has been received by young men. and its incredibly ironic, isn’t it? the nighttime tyler is so obviously wrong - he’s unbelievably destructive, untethered from real integration with others, motivated by a desire to rip down and destroy society. and then you have the identification of modern young men with that nighttime tyler, which is a real-time replication of the entire tragedy of the movie! small wonder they idolize the nighttime tyler durden, small wonder they replicate the insane and destructive masculinity he represents. they are tyler durden, actually. the fact that the nighttime tyler durden has admirers is the complete confirmation of the films critique. young men find this character and read it, poorly, as the discovery of a meaningful alternate path - the repudiation of the daytime life they hate. under the cult of the false, substitute barbarian masculinity they discover, they become barbarians themselves.

and the film dosen’t actually resolve this critique. the film is quite pessimistic actually: you have the choice between a lifeless and safe reality, or the life of a violent barbarian. and to some extent, that is the real choice that some young people navigate. so the film serves more as a diagnosis than a positional statement.

on marla: I hate the ending of the film. marla’s situation is tragic. she starts in the same place as tyler. she is wary of him, adversarial, while searching for meaning at the same time. and its sad to say that she was right to be wary. through the entire film, she’s almost invisible. when she has agency - when she’s not involved with tyler - she’s running from him, trying to get away, and seeking out community within the ordinary confines of apollonian life. you literally watch her turn away from the camera. you see her rejected from the “men’s only” fight club. when she interacts with tyler, what happens? you hear her fuck. you see her as a seductive object at the moment of her trying to kill herself. you see her literally forced onto a bus, only to be kidnapped by his army and dragged back to him against her will. she is the only person in the film that actually bears the weight of tylers actions in a personal, sustained way. and she gets nothing from it. she becomes a victim of abuse and an object under his regime.

and note how it intersects with the daytime and nighttime selves of tyler durden. the daytime self is sexless, useless, emasculated and unattractive. which is bad for tyler, but more importantly, its also useless for marla. tylers formation as a man in the daytime world is literally purposeless for her. he argues with her, awkwardly coerces her for her number, leaves her on the phone while shes expressing that shes going to kill herself. its a purposeless masculinity. and in the nighttime, the emotive, shadow self, hes disintegrated - he has nothing to offer her except his cock, which carries for a little while, but is ultimately rejected by her in the final scenes: “the sex is great, but you have real emotional problems and you need help”.

so marla is sort of the orphan of this films narrative. she’s repeatedly stripped of her agency, her needs are unmet by the society of capital (interesting parallel to tyler), she is the objectified, screamed at, kidnapped, forced around, and absued. she is an object of both capital and masculine power. she is barely able to exist as a person.

the end: speaking frankly marla should have killed tyler in the last scene. that would have redeemed so much of the films narrative as it concerns marla.

the ending is deeply unsatisfying on so many fronts. first - it positions marla as acquiescing in to tyler once again, which consequentially removes any positional statement the film could have made about what to do with all of this. whats the point? were meant to walk away thinking: were just stuck with this? had she killed tyler, the film could have argued that tyler durden as an archetype, the split male, the unintegrated male, is evil and needs to end. it could have given marla her first real moment of agency in the film. I don’t think this position is entirely uncomplicated - that ending would have also tacitly argued that it’s women’s role to fix men, that women are burdened with the task of redeeming the failed masculinity that technical society has produced. maybe there is some kind of redeeming read along the lines of “we’re in this together and we have to fix it together”. that feels watery to me. marla was deeply wronged throughout the film, and having her lock hands with her abuser in the final moments feels ridiculous. it could have argued anything but that.

and the film suggests nothing about what to do with the broken masculinity that shaped the entire plot. on the surface, daytime tyler and nighttime tyler integrating is the message. this is a jungian proverb and there’s merit there. one does need to integrate the shadow self. but what position does the film take beyond this truism? effectively nothing - tyler is as helpless to stop the destruction he caused as marla is helpless to be consumed by it. and this isn’t the actual state of the world! appollonian daylight society can change, it could change. tyler could have, for example, resolved the narrative by standing trial and explaining his case to the public. he could have offered a diagnosis of his condition to the public in a way that the public could metabolize. instead we’re left with a ridiculous image that tyler has somehow resolved the case by integrating himself.

it strikes me that the ending, really, was an expression of the cultural management we use to manage the unintegrated male, and it gives a hidden endorsement of it. integrating the self in the final moments, marla holding his hand, reads to me as “just get therapized and everything will be better”. which is not true. tyler getting therapized does effectively nothing to change the conditions that produced the split, that terrorized marla, and that ended in a massive terror attack on civil society at large. it does nothing - its a management of the symptoms in one man, strangely valorized while bombs go off in the daylight world. it is an awful, pitiful ending to the film.

my take: the film is a great diagnosis of the conditions of masculinity in civil society. it offers a real critique of appollonian, managerial, technical culture. there is no doubt that reducing women and men to human capital is completely fraught with problems. it splits the self, it rejects the human, it undermines the true emotive reality which human life runs on. it produces and engenders violence. and yet, the film does almost nothing about it. its critique has been realized only in propagation of the worst conditions the critique addresses, which is honestly speaking a total failure. in fact, it’s a far worse failure than the similar case we experience with american psycho. american psycho does the same thing to the public, it has propagated the conditions of the critique rather than addressed them, but the core difference is that patrick bateman is presented, concretely, as a movie monster, and is not redeemed by the narrative. he has no moment where the love of a woman or his own therapy work saves him. fight club, for all it’s merits, can’t seem to decide whether or not tyler durdens condition is wrong.

and it is wrong.

thanks annika 👸🏻
